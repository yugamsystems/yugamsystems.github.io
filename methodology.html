Engagement Methodology

How an engagement runs.

Six phases from the first discovery call to the 90-day post-handover review. Every step has a clear output. No black boxes, no scope creep, no surprises.

Assessment only: 2–4 weeks  ·  Full engagement: up to 32 weeks

Not every engagement runs all six phases. Where you enter depends on where you are — find out in a 30-minute conversation.

01

Discovery & Assessment

2–4 weeks

Before any strategy or tool selection, I need to understand how the business actually operates — not how it looks on an org chart. This phase is mostly listening and asking uncomfortable questions.

Stakeholder interviews
3–5 days
C-suite, ops leads, department heads, and frontline staff. The people closest to the work know where the real friction is. Leadership knows where the pressure is coming from. Both matter.
Business & operations mapping
2–3 days
Revenue model, cost drivers, key processes, and decision points. Where do decisions slow down or get made twice? Where does knowledge disappear when someone leaves?
Technology & systems audit
2–3 days
Current stack, data flows, integration points, vendor contracts. What exists, what it actually does, and what it's costing. Avoids recommending tools the client already has under a different name.
Data audit
3–5 days
Where does data live, who owns it, how clean is it, and who can access it? This is the step that reveals the hidden blockers. Data problems discovered in Phase 3 are expensive. Discovered here, they're solvable.
Opportunity mapping
2–3 days
Structured identification of AI candidates across all business units. Each scored on impact, feasibility, data availability, and time to value. Produces a ranked list — not a wish list.
Go/no-go recommendation
2–3 days
An honest assessment: where to start, where not to start, and why. Includes a preliminary cost-benefit, proposed sequence, and a clear statement of what would need to be true for this to succeed. If it's not the right time, I'll say so.
02

Strategy & Planning

2–4 weeks

With the assessment complete, this phase converts findings into a committed plan. Use cases are selected, tools evaluated, architecture drafted, and the governance framework set up before any build work starts.

Overlaps with Phase 1 end
AI strategy design
1 week
Which use cases, in which order, with which approach. Build vs. buy vs. configure for each. Sequenced by dependencies — some things have to happen before others.
Vendor & tool evaluation
3–5 days
Shortlist, structured scoring against your specific requirements, demos where needed. The evaluation criteria come from the assessment — not from vendor marketing. Avoids picking tools based on brand recognition.
Architecture design
3–5 days
How AI connects to existing systems. Data pipelines, integration points, security model, and access controls. Designed before any code is written or any vendor is contracted.
Business case & full cost model
2–3 days
Total cost of implementation — not just software licences. Includes internal time, data preparation, change management, training, and ongoing maintenance. The number most vendors don't show you.
Baseline measurement
1–2 days
Capture the before numbers now — hours spent per task, error rate, cost per unit, response time. Without a baseline established before the build, there's no credible ROI number at the end.
Risk register
1–2 days
Technical risks, data risks, change management risks, vendor dependency risks. Each with a rated likelihood, impact, and mitigation plan. A living document — updated throughout the engagement.
Governance & compliance review
1–2 days
Data privacy (GDPR, local regulations), model transparency requirements, bias audit checklist, and audit trail design. Regulatory constraints found here cost a few days to resolve. Found in Phase 3, they can stop a build cold.
Stakeholder & change management plan
1–2 days
Who needs to know what, when, and how. Resistance mapping — who is likely to push back and why. Communication cadence. Executive sponsorship alignment. Most AI implementations fail not because the technology doesn't work, but because the people piece wasn't planned.
03

Build & Pilot

8–16 weeks

The longest and most variable phase. Duration depends on the starting state of the data, the complexity of integrations, and how much custom build is required versus configuration. Iterative throughout — not a one-shot delivery.

Data preparation
2–4 weeks
Cleaning, structuring, labelling, and connecting the data the AI system needs. The step most engagements underestimate. Data in poor shape adds weeks. The assessment tells us this upfront — the work is planned for accordingly.
Solution development
4–8 weeks
Tool configuration, workflow automation build, custom integration where needed. Iterative — working software is shown to internal users early, not at the end. Feedback is captured and applied in-cycle.
Internal QA & testing
1–2 weeks
Structured testing before any real users touch it. Edge cases, failure modes, data quality issues, and integration break points. The test cases are written in Phase 2 when the architecture is designed.
Controlled pilot
2–4 weeks
Deploy to one team or one process first. Measure against the baseline established in Phase 2. Capture everything that works and everything that doesn't. A small-scope pilot failure costs a few weeks. A full-scale launch failure costs months.
Post-pilot iteration
1–2 weeks
Fix what the pilot revealed. Document what changed and why. Decision gate before scaling: does the pilot evidence justify full rollout, or does the design need to change?
Hypercare period
2–4 weeks
Intensive support immediately after go-live. When systems go live in production, things break in ways that testing didn't catch. Planned intensive support during this window contains the damage and resolves issues before they become incidents.
04

Measurement & Governance

2–3 weeks

Runs in parallel with the Build phase. The reporting infrastructure, dashboards, and governance framework are built while the AI system is being developed — so they're live on day one of production, not retrofitted afterwards.

Runs in parallel with Phase 3
Executive dashboard
3–5 days
Live view of AI performance metrics for leadership. What's running, what it's returning, where it's flagging. Built to the decisions that leadership needs to make — not the metrics that are easy to track.
Automated alerts & anomaly detection
2–3 days
System alerts when performance drops below agreed thresholds. Catches model drift, data quality issues, and integration failures before they surface as business problems.
Reporting cadence & templates
2–3 days
Weekly operational report, monthly executive report. Templates set up so the client can run these without external help after handover.
Data governance framework
2–3 days
Ongoing data ownership, access controls, quality standards, and refresh cadence. AI systems degrade over time when nobody owns data quality. This sets the structure that prevents that.
ROI documentation
1–2 days
Formal before/after comparison against the baseline metrics captured in Phase 2. The number that goes into the executive handover and justifies the next phase of investment.
05

Training & Enablement

4–8 weeks

The most commonly underinvested phase. A working AI system that your team doesn't use is a failed AI implementation. This phase is about making the change stick — not just delivering training sessions.

Role-based needs assessment
1 week
What does a finance analyst need to know vs. a customer service agent vs. an executive sponsor? Different roles need different things. Generic AI training is expensive and ineffective.
SOP rewrite
1–2 weeks
Update standard operating procedures to reflect the new AI-augmented way of working. Without updated SOPs, people revert to old habits within weeks of training ending.
Training material development
1 week
Role-specific playbooks, quick reference guides, and worked examples from the actual deployed system — not generic demos. Materials built for the tool your team is using, not an idealised version of it.
Executive briefings
2–3 sessions
Strategy-level sessions for leadership. The focus is on governance decisions, what to watch on the dashboard, and how to interpret the numbers — not on operating the tool itself.
Team workshops
2–4 weeks
Hands-on, role-specific sessions. Participants work through tasks on the actual system using their own real work — not fictional examples. The only training that transfers to day-to-day use.
Super-user programme
1 week setup, ongoing
Identify 2–3 internal champions per team who receive deeper training and become the first line of support after handover. Sustains adoption without ongoing external dependency. The single most effective way to make change stick.
Adoption measurement
4–6 weeks ongoing
Track actual system usage rates — not training completion. The common failure mode: people attend training, then don't use the tool. Usage data is reviewed weekly for the first month post-training with a structured response plan if adoption drops.
06

Handover & Close

2–3 weeks

The engagement closes only when the client can operate independently. That means named internal owners, working documentation, and a clear escalation path — not a folder of files and a goodbye call.

Documentation package
1 week
Full runbooks, architecture diagrams, vendor contacts, maintenance schedules, and troubleshooting guides. Everything needed to operate the system without calling anyone.
Internal ownership transfer
2–3 days
Named internal owner for each system — not a team, a person. Clear escalation path. Vendor relationship contacts transferred. The documentation means nothing if nobody knows they own it.
Executive handover session
Half day
Formal close with leadership. What was built, what it returned against baseline, what to watch for, what the 90-day plan looks like. ROI documentation presented and signed off.
90-day post-engagement review
1 session
One structured follow-up call 90 days after close. Checks whether the system is running as expected, whether adoption held, and whether any drift has crept in. The most common failure mode is not visible at handover — it appears 6–8 weeks later when no one is watching.
Optional retainer scope
As agreed
If ongoing support is warranted, the scope, SLA, and pricing are defined clearly and separately from the main engagement. No ambiguity about what's included after close.
