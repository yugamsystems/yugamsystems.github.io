Engagement Methodology

How an engagement runs.

Six phases from the first discovery call to the 90-day post-handover review. Every step has a clear output. No black boxes, no scope creep, no surprises.

Assessment only: 2–4 weeks  ·  Full engagement: up to 32 weeks

Not every engagement runs all six phases. Where you enter depends on where you are — find out in a 30-minute conversation.

01

Discovery & Assessment

2–4 weeks

Before any strategy or tool selection, I need to understand how the business actually operates — not how it looks on an org chart. This phase is mostly listening and asking uncomfortable questions.

Stakeholder interviews
3–5 days
C-suite to frontline — the people closest to the work know where the friction is; leadership knows where the pressure comes from.
Business & operations mapping
2–3 days
Revenue model, cost drivers, key processes, and decision points — where things slow down, get made twice, or disappear when someone leaves.
Technology & systems audit
2–3 days
Current stack, data flows, vendor contracts — what exists, what it actually does, and what it costs.
Data audit
3–5 days
Where data lives, who owns it, how clean it is — hidden blockers found here cost days to fix; found in Phase 3, they cost months.
Opportunity mapping
2–3 days
AI candidates across all business units, scored on impact, feasibility, data availability, and time to value — a ranked list, not a wish list.
Go/no-go recommendation
2–3 days
Where to start, where not to, roughly what it costs, and what would need to be true for it to succeed — including an honest read if the timing is wrong.
02

Strategy & Planning

2–4 weeks

With the assessment complete, this phase converts findings into a committed plan. Use cases are selected, tools evaluated, architecture drafted, and the governance framework set up before any build work starts.

Overlaps with Phase 1 end
AI strategy design
1 week
Which use cases, in which order, build vs. buy vs. configure — sequenced by dependencies.
Vendor & tool evaluation
3–5 days
Shortlist and structured scoring against your specific requirements — not vendor marketing.
Architecture design
3–5 days
How AI connects to existing systems — data pipelines, integration points, security model, and access controls — designed before any vendor is contracted.
Business case & full cost model
2–3 days
Total implementation cost: licences, internal time, data prep, change management, training, and ongoing maintenance.
Baseline measurement
1–2 days
Capture the before numbers now — without a pre-build baseline, there's no credible ROI figure at the end.
Risk register
1–2 days
Technical, data, change management, and vendor risks — each rated and given a mitigation plan before the build starts.
Governance & compliance review
1–2 days
Data privacy, audit trail design, and bias checklist — regulatory gaps found here take days to fix; found in Phase 3, they stop the build.
Stakeholder & change management plan
1–2 days
Who needs to know what, when, and how — resistance mapping, communication cadence, and executive sponsorship alignment.
03

Build & Pilot

8–16 weeks

The longest and most variable phase. Duration depends on the starting state of the data, the complexity of integrations, and how much custom build is required versus configuration. Iterative throughout — not a one-shot delivery.

Data preparation
2–4 weeks
Cleaning, structuring, and connecting the data the AI system needs — the step most engagements underestimate.
Solution development
4–8 weeks
Tool configuration, workflow automation, and custom integration — iterative, with working software shown to users early.
Internal QA & testing
1–2 weeks
Structured testing for edge cases, failure modes, and integration break points before any real users are involved.
Controlled pilot
2–4 weeks
One team, one process, measured against the Phase 2 baseline — a pilot failure costs weeks; a launch failure costs months.
Post-pilot iteration
1–2 weeks
Fix what the pilot revealed, document what changed, and decide: scale or redesign?
Hypercare period
2–4 weeks
Intensive support in the first weeks after go-live, when production reveals what testing didn't catch.
04

Measurement & Governance

2–3 weeks

Runs in parallel with the Build phase. The reporting infrastructure, dashboards, and governance framework are built while the AI system is being developed — so they're live on day one of production, not retrofitted afterwards.

Runs in parallel with Phase 3
Executive dashboard
3–5 days
Live AI performance metrics built to the decisions leadership needs to make — not the metrics that are easy to track.
Automated alerts & anomaly detection
2–3 days
System alerts when performance drops below threshold — catches model drift and data issues before they become business problems.
Reporting cadence & templates
2–3 days
Weekly operational and monthly executive reports, templated so the client runs them independently after handover.
Data governance framework
2–3 days
Ongoing data ownership, access controls, and quality standards — AI systems degrade when nobody owns the data.
ROI documentation
1–2 days
Formal before/after comparison against the Phase 2 baseline — the number that goes into the executive handover.
05

Enable

4–8 weeks

The most commonly underinvested phase. A working AI system that your team doesn't use is a failed AI implementation. This phase is about making the change stick — not just delivering training sessions.

Role-based needs assessment
1 week
Different roles need different things — generic AI training is expensive and doesn't stick.
SOP rewrite
1–2 weeks
Updated SOPs for the AI-augmented way of working — without them, people revert to old habits within weeks.
Training material development
1 week
Role-specific playbooks and worked examples from the actual deployed system — not generic demos.
Executive briefings
2–3 sessions
Leadership sessions focused on governance decisions and dashboard interpretation — not on operating the tool.
Team workshops
2–4 weeks
Hands-on, role-specific sessions on the actual system, using each participant's own real work.
Super-user programme
1 week setup, ongoing
2–3 internal champions per team trained more deeply to sustain adoption without ongoing external dependency.
Adoption measurement
4–6 weeks ongoing
Usage rates tracked weekly — training completion doesn't tell you if anyone is actually using the tool.
06

Handover & Close

2–3 weeks

The engagement closes only when the client can operate independently. That means named internal owners, working documentation, and a clear escalation path — not a folder of files and a goodbye call.

Documentation package
1 week
Runbooks, architecture diagrams, vendor contacts, and maintenance schedules — everything needed to operate without calling anyone.
Internal ownership transfer
2–3 days
Named internal owner for each system — not a team, a person — with a clear escalation path and transferred vendor contacts.
Executive handover session
Half day
Formal close: what was built, what it returned against baseline, what to watch, and what comes next.
90-day post-engagement review
1 session
One structured follow-up call at day 90 — the most common failure mode isn't visible at handover, it surfaces 6–8 weeks later.
Optional retainer scope
As agreed
If ongoing support is warranted, scope, SLA, and pricing defined clearly and separately from the main engagement.
