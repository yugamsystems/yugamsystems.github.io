# 66% got faster. 34% changed how they work.

*Yugam Weekly — June 27, 2026*

---

Deloitte surveyed 3,235 leaders across 24 countries this year. Two numbers worth paying attention to:

**66%** of organizations report productivity gains from AI.
**34%** say AI has deeply transformed how their business operates.

That gap — between "faster" and "different" — is the gap I spent 15 years watching organizations fall into, long before AI.

At KPMG, I saw it with governance systems. Companies would buy the compliance tool but not redesign the approval process. The tool worked. The organization didn't change. At Meta, I saw it with operations tooling across Singapore, Dublin, and Hyderabad. Teams that adopted the tool without redesigning the workflow just did the same broken process faster.

AI is the same pattern, larger scale.

**What the 34% did differently:**

**They made sure two people could pull the same number.**
This sounds trivial. It isn't. In the assessments I've built, I ask a version of this question: "If two leaders pulled last month's revenue, would they get the same answer?" The majority say no. If your AI is learning from data that your own team doesn't agree on, it will produce answers nobody trusts. Fix this before you buy anything.

**They documented the process before they automated it.**
On a manufacturing floor, you don't automate a station until you've mapped every step, every exception, every handoff. I learned this at Vedanta, running production lines. The same principle applies to AI: if the process lives in one person's head, the AI has nothing to learn from. The 34% wrote it down first.

**They figured out who was ready and who wasn't — before launch.**
The World Economic Forum published research this month identifying five distinct readiness profiles in the workforce. Enthusiasts, pragmatists, skeptics, the anxious, and the resistant. Each needs a different approach. The 34% didn't send a company-wide email announcing AI. They mapped their people and planned accordingly.
